Machine Learning

Machine learning is the study of computer algorithms that can automatically improve through experience. The term machine learning was coined in 1959 by Arthur Samuel, Tom M. Mitchell later provided a more formal definition:
"A computer program is said to learn from experience (E) with respect to some class of tasks (T) and performance measure (P) if its performance at tasks in (T), as measured by (P), improves with experience (E)." - Mitchell, T. (1997). Machine Learning. McGraw Hill
Algorithms are given a large amount of sample data to 'train' to find patterns and correlations. After doing so the program can make predictions for desired outputs. They do this by creating neural networks.
To simplify it a set of already completed data and takes an input layer where it gathers all information it has, then processes it in a "hidden" layer which applies different equations to make the final output layer. The program does these hundreds of times and compares each output to the desired output gaining accuracy over time. it shares similarities without own human brains and as such gained the title "neural networks"
It's already used in programs everywhere. for example, email filtering can see what users usually report as spam and find trends to automatically flag new emails as spam. recording what the user declares as spam and then extrapolating the data to apply to later incoming emails This is great because creating a conventional algorithm would be very difficult. Another feature is the programs ability to keep up to date. a conventional algorithm would have to be manually updated, but often machine learning programs like the email filter can just receive more sample data to increase capabilities.
It's being taken further in image recognition services. Social media platforms like Facebook use it to automatically tag users. allowing easy notifying of those included in photos.
One of the most futuristic current uses is in self-driving cars. using image recognition to identify hazards, cars roadsigns and more. please see the relevant page on our website. being a core part of Artificial intelligence, machine learning is necessary for these inventions to be used.
Unfourtantley the biggest current hindrance to its widespread usage is obtaining experience, its sample data. Sample data can be 'bad data' in multiple ways, such as not enough data, non-representative data, poor quality data, irrelevant features. This was seen in multiple facial recognition software where, due to sampling a primarily white collection of people, the facial detection had a much harder time differentiating from darker-skinned individuals. it is likely however to get better over time and as algorithms get made smarter and fewer data would be necessary.
In the future, we will continue to see Machine learning in the medical field where it is currently being used to assist doctors in diagnosis. In one example a machine learning algorithm achieved an accuracy of 97% making this a powerful tool for doctors. Due to the nature of machine learning the better it gets the more people simply disregard its use as "just an alogorythim" as machine learning at its best is very simple and quick to use. No one is amazed at YouTube's recommendation feature even though the technology behind it is adapting millions of key points of data. This could lead to a future where machine learning may be just an automatic assumption, Always used but never considered.

The Cloud: Cloud Computing & Technology

What is it, what does it do?

Cloud Computing has played an integral part in the way we use technology and the internet in the modern age. ‘The Cloud’ referring to cloud computing, has become the foundation of how technology, systems, applications and data storage is interacted with, from an individual to conglomerate scale (Ranger 2018; Shmatko et al. 2020). Originally the benefit of the cloud was its flexibility and convenience to remotely store data, however, as technology has advanced so too did the capabilities, insofar as supplying access to substantial remote processing power all as long as there is reliable access to the internet (Techquickie 2014; Shmatko et al. 2020; Cloudflare 2021). This enhanced processing power is only possible due to Cloud Service Providers (CSP) the likes of Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud Platform and Alibaba Cloud (the top 4 cloud providers) building the IT infrastructure required for the cloud to function. This done through a network of servers (a series of connected computers) allows individuals and companies to gain access to infrastructure and resources which normally would require high upfront costs for hardware/software, physical space, data; storage, maintenance, security and recovery, these being some of the limiting factors for flexible scalability (Techquickie 2014; Ecourse Review 2017; Simplilearn 2020; Cloudflare 2021). The benefits of the cloud is that it subsides these upfront costs with a ‘Pay as you go’ model, so when required an individual or company can purchase the certain amount of resources needed for the job or time period whilst having the ability to increase or decrease the amount of resources required, be-it extra storage or processing it is all available on demand and enables great flexibility and scalability for the consumer (Techquickie 2014; Ecourse Review 2017; Simplilearn 2020). Furthermore, the convenience provided by CPS’s through the ‘pay as you go’ model reduces the costs for hardware and software, enables effective data recovery in case a data center is compromised the consumer data is backed up at multiple locations, improved data security, reliable technical support and finally greater mobility and accessibility (Techquickie 2014; Ranger 2018; Shmatko et al. 2020; Simplilearn 2020; Cloudflare 2021). To expand, the mobility and accessibility provided by the cloud is one of its core benefits, the ability to use applications, services, retrieve and store data all whilst being on the move is very valuable more-so in our current climate then before, being able to work from home or remotely allows the consumer to have more freedoms in who they work with, how work is can be conducted and where they can work, removing the limitations of the past. Furthermore , the accessibility of the cloud means that access to applications, services or data can all be done through smaller or lower powered devices all in the spirit of being cost effective to the consumer (Techquickie 2014; Shmatko et al. 2020; Simplilearn 2020; Cloudflare 2021).

Currently companies such as Amazon through Amazon Web Services offer over 200 services through the cloud these encompassing the 3 most common delivery models of cloud computing; Software as a Service (SaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS) and Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), each giving the developer different scales of control in how they utilise the cloud (Ecourse Review 2017; Ranger 2018 Fireship 2019; Amazon Web Services 2021; Cloudflare 2021). SaaS refers to the common public cloud (able to be accessed by anyone) services such as data storage through Google Drive, Microsoft OneDrive or Dropbox, this also includes video streaming services the likes of Netflix or Youtube and in regards to processing power examples include Google Workspace (Google Docs, Sheets, Slide, etc.) or Microsoft 365. PaaS coincides more with software or application development, as it offers greater control to the developer granting them access to more specialised tools for creating testing and maintaining software. Finally IaaS is used mostly in System/IT Administration as these can provide access to computing infrastructure enabling the use of virtual machines or private storage storage only accessible specifically by that company alongside even more specialised tools (Ecourse Review 2017; Fireship 2019; Simplilearn 2020; Cloudflare 2021). These three cloud deployment models are not the only models but are the most widely used, with the increased adaptation and accelerated advancement of this technology more and more models are being created (such as Function as a Service (FaaS)), with more widespread use of cloud computing having been made possible, as other fields of IT and technology that of Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence and even Cloud Gaming benefit and make strides as they continue implementing and employing the power of the cloud and the attached economic benefits. (Techquickie 2014; fireship 2019; Shmatko et al. 2020).

Future Impact

The impact of Cloud Technology has risen exponentially over the decade with companies and individuals alike utilising the cloud in their everyday services and internet usage. As this technology has continued to develop the financial costs associated continue to decrease whilst the benefits have increased, with the switch to cloud services removing the costs associated with having on-premises of the physical space, hardware and accompanying software, the staff required to install, maintain, update and recovery the it infrastructure, and lastly data storage and security all being key factors which are involved in maintaining a digital workplace (Shmatko et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021; Cloud Flare 2021). The switch to the cloud and reliance of Cloud Service Provider eliminates the costs associated with having a robust it infrastructure as the consumer only pays for the resources they require be-it data storage, processing power or more complex systems, the consumer no longer needs to maintain their own infrastructure (Techquickie 2014; Ecourse Review 2017; Simplilearn 2020; Cloudflare 2021). This all being a benefit to the scalability for a consumer as they have on demand access to increase of decrease the amount of resources which they use as they require, furthermore, this including the outsourcing their required infrastructure in to their desired scale (Ecourse Review 2017; Ranger 2018 Fireship 2019; Amazon Web Services 2021; Cloudflare 2021). Outside of the 3 common cloud delivery models; Software as a Service (SaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS) and Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) a new model has gained popularity as it gives more specified outsourcing of cloud infrastructure. Called Function as a Service (FaaS) alternatively named ‘Serverless Computing’, by breaking down cloud services into smaller components this model will only run when needed in addition to scaling in relation to the amount of demand of service (Cloudflare 2021). Though being named “serverless” FaaS services still operate on servers just not their own dedicated machines as the consumer would not have acquired a dedicated server but a section of a server (Cloudflare 2021).

The effect of FaaS and the 3 cloud deployment models and potential newer methods is very positive, more-so in the added reduction of costs as consumers are able to choose the appropriate model associated with their needs (Shmatko et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021; Cloud Flare 2021). However, with more options comes ‘Choice/Option Paralysis’ defined by Lexico in association with the Oxford Dictionary (2021) as “The inability to make a decision when presented with a wide range of choices'' as consumers have to decide on which service may apply to their specific use case, compounded with a lack of understanding of the differing cloud delivery models due to the recency of cloud technology, specifically the drastic advancement of the technology (Shmatko et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021; Cloudflare 2021). Amidst this a new IT profession has become more valuable than ever, these being ‘Cloud Architects’ (Shesshy 2019; Shmatko et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021). Cloud Architects are given the task of facilitating the consumer's switch to the cloud, providing the oversight for the architecture, design and implementation of the appropriate cloud model to the desired service in addition to monitoring and analysis of the effectiveness of the service (Shesshy 2019; Shmatko et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021). Alongside other members of a ‘Technology team’ ensure that cloud computing is able to be implemented successfully and used effectively all whilst being financially viable for the consumer (Shesshy 2019).
